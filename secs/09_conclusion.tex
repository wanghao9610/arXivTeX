\section{Conclusion}

\method provides a compact starting point for public research papers. The class
keeps reusable presentation logic in \texttt{main.cls}, while the paper
source stays focused on content. The directory structure mirrors a larger paper
project: sections, figures, tables, algorithms, and references each have a clear
home.

\parahead{Limitations}
State the conditions under which the method is expected to work less well:
data regimes it was not evaluated on, assumptions the derivation relies on,
and any computational cost that would make it impractical at a larger scale.
Naming these limitations directly, rather than leaving them implicit, gives
readers an accurate picture of where the method can be safely applied.

\parahead{Future Work}
Use this paragraph to point beyond the current paper: follow-up questions the
experiments raised but did not resolve, settings the method has not yet been
tested in, and extensions that would address the limitations above. Keeping
this separate from the main conclusion helps readers distinguish established
findings from directions that still need evidence. A useful habit is to
revisit this paragraph once a follow-up project starts, since the questions
that looked most promising while writing the paper are not always the ones
that turn out to matter once new results come in. Track these open questions
alongside the codebase, not just in the paper text, so they stay visible to
collaborators after submission.
